Hands Off Our Rides

The QLD government wants to ban kids from ebikes and e-scooters. We say no.

What they're doing

The Law, Plain and Simple

Queensland's new proposed laws would make it illegal for anyone under 16 to ride an ebike or e-scooter — even on footpaths, even with a parent watching, even in your own suburb.

Over 16? You'll need a learner's licence just to ride one. Speed on footpaths is capped at 10km/h — which is barely a fast walk. Go too fast and police can seize and destroy your ride. Your parents can even be fined for letting you use one.

Why it's wrong

This Law Is a Joke

🚲

Regular bikes aren't exempt

You can already get fined for going over 10km/h on a footpath on a normal pushbike. So now kids can't ride anything at a normal pace anywhere near a road or footpath without risking a fine. Great plan.

🏫

Kids lose independence

Ebikes and scooters give kids a safe, cheap way to get around — to school, to friends, to sport. Take that away and they're stuck waiting for mum or dad to drive them everywhere.

🌏

Punishes the wrong people

The real problem is illegal high-powered bikes doing 60km/h. But instead of targeting those, the law punishes every normal kid on a legal 25km/h scooter. Backwards.

💸

A licence to ride a scooter?

Making 16-year-olds get a learner's licence to ride an e-scooter is absurd. You don't need a licence to ride a horse or a skateboard. This is just bureaucracy for bureaucracy's sake.

The school run

Families Depend on Them

Ebikes and e-scooters have become one of the most practical ways for kids to get to school — cheap, green, and no parking nightmare for parents. These aren't fringe devices anymore. Families all across Queensland rely on them every single day for the morning school run.

For plenty of working parents, a kid riding an ebike to school means one less trip, one less car on the road, and one less scheduling headache every morning. It gives kids independence and gets them there safely without mum or dad having to drop everything.

This law wipes all of that out overnight. Under-16s lose access entirely. Older teens need a learner's licence just to ride one. Families who've built their whole morning routine around these devices are just supposed to figure something else out — and the government doesn't care.

Many Queensland families rely on ebikes and e-scooters to get their kids to school every day. This law doesn't just affect riders — it throws thousands of family routines into chaos.
Not all ebikes are the same

Draw the Right Line

Not every ebike is the same and the law should reflect that. E dirt bikes — built for speed, off-road performance and power — are a different story in public spaces. Limiting where and how those are ridden in public areas makes sense. Nobody is arguing otherwise.

But cruiser style ebikes and e-scooters are completely different. They're built for getting around, not tearing up footpaths. They're how families commute, how kids get to school, how people avoid adding another car to already congested roads. Treating them the same as a high-powered dirt bike is lazy lawmaking that punishes responsible riders for someone else's behaviour.

And consider this — when fuel shortages hit and petrol stations run dry, ebikes and e-scooters become essential. People still need to get to work, get kids to school, and get groceries. An ebike charged at home doesn't depend on a functioning fuel supply chain. Banning or over-restricting them doesn't just hurt convenience — in a crisis it cuts people off from basic transport.

Regulate the right riders, not every rider. A cruiser ebike doing 20km/h to school is not the same as an e dirt bike doing 60km/h through a park.
The speed fine issue

Yes, You Can Already Be Fined on a Normal Bike

Here's something most people don't know — in Queensland, riding a pushbike too fast on a shared path can already get you a fine. The 10km/h limit in pedestrian zones applies to cyclists too. That's slower than most people jog.

Adding the same limit to ebikes and scooters doesn't make anyone safer — it just means more opportunities for police to write tickets to teenagers doing literally nothing dangerous.

A fit adult walks at 5km/h. A slow jog is 8km/h. The government wants your kid's ride capped at 10km/h. On a device built to go 25km/h.
